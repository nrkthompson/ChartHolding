Nova Scotia

February 15, 2021

Nova Scotia reported one new case of COVID-19 on February 15, leaving the province with a total of 10 active cases -- up from 8 one week ago. As of February 10, travel between Nova Scotia and Prince Edward Island is permitted without the requirement to self-isolate when entering or returning home from either province. Most non-essential businesses, such as fitness centres and retail stores, can remain open across the province at 75% capacity and with hygienic measures in place. Businesses and organizations can host in-person events and activities, including arts, cultural, and sporting events, festivals, weddings, funerals, and other social events, with up to 100 participants indoors and 150 participants outdoors. The province will remain in a state of emergency until at least February 21.

February 8, 2021

Nova Scotia has reported 5 new cases of COVID-19 in the past 7 days. There are currently 8 active cases in the province, down from 10 one week ago. As of February 8, fitness establishments, such as gyms and yoga studios, are permitted to reopen at 75% capacity with hygienic measures in place; and businesses and organizations can now host in-person events and activities, including arts, cultural, and sporting events, festivals, weddings, funerals, and other social events, with up to 100 participants indoors, and 150 outdoors. The province remains in a state of emergency.

February 1, 2021

Nova Scotia reported 8 new cases of COVID-19 in the past 7 days. There are currently 10 active cases in the province. Ongoing health measures are mainly limited to hygienic and physical distancing guidelines, and a limit of 60 participants (increased from 50 as of February 1) for public gatherings, such as cultural and sporting events. The province remains in a state of emergency.

January 25, 2021

Nova Scotia has reported only one positive test result for the virus since January 22, the same day that the province identified one case each of the UK and South African COVID-19 variants. There are currently 15 active cases in the province, the lowest tally since early November. There are no Nova Scotians in hospital due to the virus. Some restrictions on sports, arts and culture were eased on January 25. Sports teams are now permitted to play games against each other without spectators; art and theatre performances can also take place without an audience.

January 18, 2021

On January 18, Nova Scotia confirmed 4 new cases of COVID-19. One of the new cases has been attributed to a student at Mount Saint Vincent University in Halifax; other recent cases have also been linked to universities. The province’s number of active cases dropped to 29 as of January 18. Catering and licensed liquor establishments in Halifax and Hants county were permitted to re-open for dine-in service on January 4; these localities were previously under tighter restrictions than the rest of the province due to a concentration of cases in the Greater Halifax Area.

December 21, 2020

Nova Scotia reported two new cases of COVID-19 on December 21, leaving the province with a total of 38 active cases. New restrictions came into effect across the province on December 21, including a limit of 10 people at household gatherings. All retailers in Nova Scotia must now limit the number of staff and customers in their stores to 25% of their legal capacity, and catering establishments must close by 10 pm. The Halifax Regional Municipality (HRM) and neighbouring Hants County, which were previously under stricter public health rules, are now under the same restrictions as the rest of the province. As of December 21, Nova Scotians are no longer being advised to avoid non-essential travel to the HRM. Gyms, libraries and museums can also reopen in the HRM, and long-term care homes will start allowing family visitors and two designated caregivers. Restrictions that came into effect on December 21 will last until at least January 10.

December 14, 2020

Nova Scotia reported a total of 59 active cases of COVID-19 on December 13, down from 88 active cases one week ago. As of December 14, there are zero hospitalizations in the province due to the virus. On December 12, the premier announced that the province would extend its winter break for public school students in order to minimize the spread of COVID-19. Schools will now close on December 18, rather than December 22, and reopen to students on January 11 instead of January 4. The Halifax Regional Municipality (HRM) and neighboring Hants County remain under stricter public health rules than the rest of the province. Nova Scotians are advised to avoid all non-essential travel to the HRM and to other Atlantic provinces. Public gatherings in the HRM are limited to 5 people, and catering establishments are restricted to take-out and delivery services.

December 7, 2020

Nova Scotia reported a total of 88 active cases on December 6, down from 126 one week ago. The Halifax Regional Municipality (HRM) and neighbouring Hants County, the provinceÕs hardest hit regions, have remained under stricter public health rules -- in addition to the stricter province-wide restrictions that were originally introduced on November 26. Nova Scotians are advised to avoid all non-essential travel to the Halifax area and to other Atlantic provinces. Public gatherings in the HRM are limited to 5 people, and catering establishments are restricted to take-out and delivery services. Province-wide measures are mostly advisory in nature, but do include a ban nursing homes visits -- with the exception of volunteers.

November 30, 2020

Nova Scotia reported 11 new cases on November 29, leaving the province with a total of 126 active cases -- up from 44 active cases last week. Public officials moved to place Halifax and neighboring Hants County, the provinceÕs hardest hit regions, under stricter public health rules last week -- followed by additional province-wide restrictions on November 26. The premier said on November 24 that the province needs to Òflip the switch on COVID-19 right now [...] COVID-19 is moving quickly in Halifax, and we need to stop it from spreading further into Nova Scotia.Ó Nova Scotians are being advised to avoid all non-essential travel to the Halifax Regional Municipality (HRM) and to other Atlantic provinces. Public gatherings in HRM are limited to 5 people, and catering establishments are restricted to take-out and delivery services. Province-wide measures include the restriction of visiting hours for nursing homes, with the exception of volunteers.

November 23, 2020

Nova Scotia reported 11 new cases of COVID-19 on November 22, the provinceÕs largest single-day increase since May 4, leaving the province with a total of 44 active cases. This increase in cases has prompted officials to place Halifax and neighbouring Hants County, the provinceÕs hardest hit regions, under stricter public health rules that will last until at least December 21. Gatherings without physical distancing will be limited to five people for both regions, and households will only be allowed to host up to five visitors at a time. Larger indoor gatherings, such as sports games, cultural events, religious gatherings, weddings, and funerals, must be limited to 50% of the venueÕs capacity -- up to a maximum of 100 people. The premier has stated previously that he Òwill not hesitate, if required, to shut down this economy in sections [...]Ó if new cases continue to emerge at the current rate.

November 16, 2020

Nova Scotia reported six new cases of COVID-19 on November 15, the provinceÕs largest single-day increase since May -- followed by another two new cases the following day, leaving the province with a total of 21 active cases. Nova Scotia Health has followed up on these reports by issuing multiple advisories of potential COVID-19 exposure around the province, while the premier has expressed concern over the possibility that Òpeople are going about their lives as if we are not still in the midst of a pandemic.Ó Nova Scotia health officials have reported that, of the provinceÕs 1,142 confirmed cases to date, 1,056 of them have been resolved. There have been 65 deaths as a result of COVID-19 in Nova Scotia.

As of November 1, most hockey arenas and sports venues in the Halifax region have been allowed to increase their spectator capacity from 50 to 100. Nova Scotia reported two new cases of COVID-19 on October 31, bringing the provinceÕs total active cases up to 13. October 5, 2020

As of October 2, Nova Scotia has three active cases of COVID-19, with one new case identified on October 1. The province has also renewed its state of emergency, effective from October 4 until October 18, unless the government decides to terminate or extend it. The province of Nova Scotia has provided residents with guidance for Thanksgiving gatherings and reminded them of applicable restrictions on gatherings. People are allowed to gather in close social groups of up to 10 without physical distancing gatherings, and 250 people indoors with social distancing. Nova Scotians are being strongly encouraged to celebrate Thanksgiving with family and friends from their consistent group of 10. Anyone who leaves the Atlantic Bubble for Thanksgiving celebrations must self-isolate for 14 days upon returning to Atlantic Canada.

September 22, 2020

As of September 20, Nova Scotia has reported no new cases of COVID-19 for the 13th day in a row. This also marks the third day with no active cases in the province. On September 18, the province announced that it is easing restrictions by increasing the gathering limit for organized sports and performing arts. The number of people allowed to participate -- including coaches, crew and anyone involved -- is now 50.

September 10, 2020

Nova Scotia’s provincial government is easing restrictions in long-term care homes to allow designated caregivers to help care for and support residents. The province announced on September 8 that designated caregivers can be family members, spouses, friends, or other support people. A designated caregiver must provide the resident with support such as personal care, mobility, or help with eating, and have had an established caregiving relationship with the resident before the COVID-19 pandemic began. Long-term care facilities will work with residents, families and decision-makers to identify up to two designated caregivers per resident. However, only one designated caregiver may visit a resident at a time. Facilities will train caregivers on public health requirements, including the use of masks and good hand and respiratory hygiene, along with facility procedures. They will also establish processes to screen caregivers when they arrive at the facility and identify them while on site.

August 24, 2020

In light of the province’s favourable epidemiological situation, Nova Scotia’s government has begun to consider expanding the Atlantic travel bubble to include the rest of Canada--thereby allowing Canadians to travel to Nova Scotia without a mandatory 14-day quarantine upon arrival. Nova Scotia premier Stephen McNeil is particularly keen on reopening the province to the rest of Canada, saying the province’s economy “requires an open border.” There were no new cases of COVID-19 in Nova Scotia on August 24; 7 active cases remain in the province.

August 10, 2020

Nova Scotia’s emergency order, which was set to expire on August 10, has been extended until at least August 23. This is despite there being only 2 active cases in the province and no new cases since August 3.

August 4, 2020

Nova Scotia Health has released an advisory of a potential exposure to COVID-19 on July 31 at Chebucto Road HRM Park, which had previously been mistakenly reported to have occurred at the Maritime Muslim Academy playground in Halifax. The advisory is related to an individual(s) who may have been asymptomatic before visiting the playground or experiencing mild symptoms that had previously gone unnoticed. The advisory is being issued out of an abundance of caution. In addition, Nova Scotia Health is directly contacting anyone else known to be a close contact of the person(s) confirmed to have COVID-19.

July 27, 2020

From July 24, all passengers and drivers of public transport in Nova Scotia are required to wear masks and/or personal protective equipment. As of July 27, the province has reported 12 consecutive days without any new cases of COVID-19, with no active cases remaining in the province.

July 20, 2020

Starting on July 22, residents at long-term care facilities may have up to 1 visitor per resident for indoor visits, and up to 5 visitors per resident for outdoor visits. During visits, both residents and visitors are required to wear masks and maintain physical distance from one another. Residents and staff at long-term care facilities can gather in groups of up to 10 without physical distancing. Licensed hair salons in long-term care facilities can now reopen to serve residents. All passengers and drivers on public transportation will be required to wear masks as of July 24.

July 13, 2020

On July 10, the government of Nova Scotia decided to extend its state of emergency until at least July 26. As of July 13, the province has gone 4 consecutive days without reporting any new cases of COVID-19, leaving only 3 active cases in the province.

July 6, 2020

Starting July 3, travel within the Atlantic provinces (Nova Scotia, New Brunswick, Prince Edward Island, Newfoundland and Labrador) will be permitted without the requirement to self-isolate for Atlantic Canada residents. Canadians residing outside of Atlantic Canada must self-isolate for 14 days upon arriving in Nova Scotia, unless they have already self-isolated in another Atlantic province--in which case they may enter Nova Scotia without self-isolating again. Also on July 3, social events, religious gatherings, weddings, funerals, cultural events, and other mass gatherings will be allowed to take place with up to 250 people outdoors, and up to 50% capacity--or 200 people--for indoor venues.

June 26, 2020

The government of Nova Scotia is limiting mass gatherings to a maximum of 50 people for both indoor and outdoor gatherings. Public schools and pre-primary education will remain closed for the remainder of the 2019/20 school year, while daycare services and unlicensed childcare providers have been allowed to resume activities in accordance with the province’s ministry of health. Personal services – such as barbers, spas, salons, and body art establishments have been allowed to resume business, along with fitness establishments – such as gyms, yoga studios, and climbing facilities – have been allowed to reopen if they are able to maintain a physical distance of 2 metres between patrons, or a limit of ten patrons at any given time if the appropriate distance cannot be maintained. Restaurants are being allowed to operate at 50% capacity while maintaining a minimum physical distance of 2 metres between tables, or providing customers with a physical barrier between tables if this distance cannot be maintained.
