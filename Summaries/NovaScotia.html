Nova Scotia

November 23, 2020

Nova Scotia reported 11 new cases of COVID-19 on November 22, the province’s largest single-day increase since May 4, leaving the province with a total of 44 active cases. This increase in cases has prompted officials to place Halifax and neighbouring Hants County, the province’s hardest hit regions, under stricter public health rules that will last until at least December 21. Gatherings without physical distancing will be limited to five people for both regions, and households will only be allowed to host up to five visitors at a time. Larger indoor gatherings, such as sports games, cultural events, religious gatherings, weddings, and funerals, must be limited to 50% of the venue’s capacity -- up to a maximum of 100 people. The premier has stated previously that he “will not hesitate, if required, to shut down this economy in sections [...]” if new cases continue to emerge at the current rate.

November 16, 2020

Nova Scotia reported six new cases of COVID-19 on November 15, the province’s largest single-day increase since May -- followed by another two new cases the following day, leaving the province with a total of 21 active cases. Nova Scotia Health has followed up on these reports by issuing multiple advisories of potential COVID-19 exposure around the province, while the premier has expressed concern over the possibility that “people are going about their lives as if we are not still in the midst of a pandemic.” Nova Scotia health officials have reported that, of the province’s 1,142 confirmed cases to date, 1,056 of them have been resolved. There have been 65 deaths as a result of COVID-19 in Nova Scotia.

November 9, 2020

Nova Scotia reported four new cases on November 7, bringing the province’s total active cases up to 20. Nova Scotia Health has followed up on these confirmations with multiple advisories of potential COVID-19 exposure around the province. The premier has expressed concern over “the recent increase in both the number of cases and public exposure notices.”

November 2, 2020

As of November 1, most hockey arenas and sports venues in the Halifax region have been allowed to increase their spectator capacity from 50 to 100. Nova Scotia reported two new cases of COVID-19 on October 31, bringing the province’s total active cases up to 13. Health officials reported on November 2 that among the province’s 1,111 confirmed cases since March, 1,033 of them have been resolved. There have been 65 deaths as a result of COVID-19 in Nova Scotia. Health officials have indicated there are no hospitalizations due to the virus at this time.

October 26, 2020

Nova Scotia reported 1 new case of COVID-19 on October 26, with 2 recoveries reported on the same day. There are now just 5 active cases in the province. Health officials reported on October 26 that of the province’s 1,101 cases since March, 1,031 of them have been resolved. There have been 65 deaths as a result of COVID-19 in Nova Scotia. Health officials have said there are no Nova Scotians in hospital due to the virus at this time.

October 19, 2020

Two new cases of COVID-19 were confirmed in Nova Scotia on October 17, bringing the province’s total active cases up to 6. Both cases are related to travel, and located in the province's Central Zone. Meanwhile, Nova Scotia Public Health has warned about a possible exposure on a flight from Toronto to Halifax on October 15.

October 13, 2020

Three new cases of COVID-19 were identified on October 10, bringing Nova Scotia’s total active cases up to six as of October 11.Two of these cases were related to travel outside of Canada, and the third was a close contact of the other two. Nova Scotia Health Authority reported that the three new cases are not related to the recent outbreak in New Brunswick. As of October 13, Nova Scotia has not reported any new cases of COVID-19 for the last 3 days.

October 5, 2020

As of October 2, Nova Scotia has three active cases of COVID-19, with one new case identified on October 1. The province has also renewed its state of emergency, effective from October 4 until October 18, unless the government decides to terminate or extend it. The province of Nova Scotia has provided residents with guidance for Thanksgiving gatherings and reminded them of applicable restrictions on gatherings. People are allowed to gather in close social groups of up to 10 without physical distancing gatherings, and 250 people indoors with social distancing. Nova Scotians are being strongly encouraged to celebrate Thanksgiving with family and friends from their consistent group of 10. Anyone who leaves the Atlantic Bubble for Thanksgiving celebrations must self-isolate for 14 days upon returning to Atlantic Canada.

September 28, 2020

As of September 28, Nova Scotia has one active case of COVID-19. No new cases were reported for 6 consecutive days. 878 COVID-19 tests were completed in the province over the weekend, all of which came back were negative. The only known active case, an essential worker in Nova Scotia’s Western zone, remains in hospital.

September 22, 2020

As of September 20, Nova Scotia has reported no new cases of COVID-19 for the 13th day in a row. This also marks the third day with no active cases in the province. On September 18, the province announced that it is easing restrictions by increasing the gathering limit for organized sports and performing arts. The number of people allowed to participate -- including coaches, crew and anyone involved -- is now 50.

September 10, 2020

Nova Scotiaís provincial government is easing restrictions in long-term care homes to allow designated caregivers to help care for and support residents. The province announced on September 8 that designated caregivers can be family members, spouses, friends, or other support people. A designated caregiver must provide the resident with support such as personal care, mobility, or help with eating, and have had an established caregiving relationship with the resident before the COVID-19 pandemic began. Long-term care facilities will work with residents, families and decision-makers to identify up to two designated caregivers per resident. However, only one designated caregiver may visit a resident at a time. Facilities will train caregivers on public health requirements, including the use of masks and good hand and respiratory hygiene, along with facility procedures. They will also establish processes to screen caregivers when they arrive at the facility and identify them while on site.

August 24, 2020

In light of the provinceís favourable epidemiological situation, Nova Scotiaís government has begun to consider expanding the Atlantic travel bubble to include the rest of Canada--thereby allowing Canadians to travel to Nova Scotia without a mandatory 14-day quarantine upon arrival. Nova Scotia premier Stephen McNeil is particularly keen on reopening the province to the rest of Canada, saying the provinceís economy ìrequires an open border.î There were no new cases of COVID-19 in Nova Scotia on August 24; 7 active cases remain in the province.

August 17, 2020

As of August 17, Nova Scotia has four active cases of COVID-19, one of which was identified on August 16.

August 10, 2020

Nova Scotiaís emergency order, which was set to expire on August 10, has been extended until at least August 23. This is despite there being only 2 active cases in the province and no new cases since August 3.

August 4, 2020

Nova Scotia Health has released an advisory of a potential exposure to COVID-19 on July 31 at Chebucto Road HRM Park, which had previously been mistakenly reported to have occurred at the Maritime Muslim Academy playground in Halifax. The advisory is related to an individual(s) who may have been asymptomatic before visiting the playground or experiencing mild symptoms that had previously gone unnoticed. The advisory is being issued out of an abundance of caution. In addition, Nova Scotia Health is directly contacting anyone else known to be a close contact of the person(s) confirmed to have COVID-19.

July 27, 2020

From July 24, all passengers and drivers of public transport in Nova Scotia are required to wear masks and/or personal protective equipment. As of July 27, the province has reported 12 consecutive days without any new cases of COVID-19, with no active cases remaining in the province.

July 20, 2020

Starting on July 22, residents at long-term care facilities may have up to 1 visitor per resident for indoor visits, and up to 5 visitors per resident for outdoor visits. During visits, both residents and visitors are required to wear masks and maintain physical distance from one another. Residents and staff at long-term care facilities can gather in groups of up to 10 without physical distancing. Licensed hair salons in long-term care facilities can now reopen to serve residents. All passengers and drivers on public transportation will be required to wear masks as of July 24.

July 13, 2020

On July 10, the government of Nova Scotia decided to extend its state of emergency until at least July 26. As of July 13, the province has gone 4 consecutive days without reporting any new cases of COVID-19, leaving only 3 active cases in the province.

July 6, 2020

Starting July 3, travel within the Atlantic provinces (Nova Scotia, New Brunswick, Prince Edward Island, Newfoundland and Labrador) will be permitted without the requirement to self-isolate for Atlantic Canada residents. Canadians residing outside of Atlantic Canada must self-isolate for 14 days upon arriving in Nova Scotia, unless they have already self-isolated in another Atlantic province--in which case they may enter Nova Scotia without self-isolating again. Also on July 3, social events, religious gatherings, weddings, funerals, cultural events, and other mass gatherings will be allowed to take place with up to 250 people outdoors, and up to 50% capacity--or 200 people--for indoor venues.

June 26, 2020

The government of Nova Scotia is limiting mass gatherings to a maximum of 50 people for both indoor and outdoor gatherings. Public schools and pre-primary education will remain closed for the remainder of the 2019/20 school year, while daycare services and unlicensed childcare providers have been allowed to resume activities in accordance with the provinceís ministry of health. Personal services ñ such as barbers, spas, salons, and body art establishments have been allowed to resume business, along with fitness establishments ñ such as gyms, yoga studios, and climbing facilities ñ have been allowed to reopen if they are able to maintain a physical distance of 2 metres between patrons, or a limit of ten patrons at any given time if the appropriate distance cannot be maintained. Restaurants are being allowed to operate at 50% capacity while maintaining a minimum physical distance of 2 metres between tables, or providing customers with a physical barrier between tables if this distance cannot be maintained.
